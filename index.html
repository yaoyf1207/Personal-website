Air Quality & Housing Prices

Turning Chaos into Order

The Real Challenge

In most data science courses, datasets come pre-cleaned and simplified. I wanted to challenge myself with the messy reality of federal data to answer a complex question: Does air quality genuinely impact housing prices?

I consolidated sixteen separate years of raw CSV files from the EPA, FHFA, and BEA, handling non-standard missing values and sanitizing inconsistent formatting that would have broken a standard model.

Key Findings

  • Cleaner Air, Higher Prices: Better air quality (lower AQI) is associated with significantly higher housing prices
  • Independent Effect: Controlling for income and other factors, the effect remains statistically significant (coefficient: 0.389): air quality affects housing independently of wealth
  • Policy Implication: Environmental improvements generate direct asset value appreciation, providing economic justification for stricter regulations

Why This Matters to Me

This project represents my capability to manage the entire data science lifecycle, not just the final modeling stage. It demonstrates the technical resilience to wrangle unstructured information into clean, analytical format, and translate chaotic real-world data into clear, policy-relevant economic insights.

GRE Prep Web App

Product Thinking & Willing to Try

Born from Personal Frustration

As a non-native speaker preparing for the GRE, I found that existing tools to learn vocabularies were rigid. Traditional PDFs lacked interactivity, and mainstream apps didn't allow me to upload my own custom vocabulary lists.

Instead of waiting for a better tool, I decided to build one: a web app designed to help non-native speakers master GRE synonyms. I adopted a Product Manager mindset, identified a specific gap in the market for international students, and leveraged AI-assisted coding ("vibe coding") to rapidly prototype and deploy it.

Key Features

  • Smart Dashboard: Progress tracking with daily/cumulative word counts, auto-resetting daily counter
  • AI-Powered Story Generator: Creates short narratives using your recently studied words with customizable themes (e.g., "Harry Potter style")
  • Smart Excel Import: Upload custom .xlsx vocabulary files, no dependence on pre-built lists
  • Immersive Flashcards: 3D flip card interface with star-marking for difficult words
  • Privacy First: BYOK (Bring Your Own Key) system: API keys stored locally, never transmitted

Time-Series Modeling of Seasonal Precipitation

GEARS Project | Supervisor: Prof. Min Chi | NC State University

The Problem

Accurate precipitation prediction in the dry California Central Valley is vital for regional agricultural production. Farmers need to know when extreme rainfall will hit, not just seasonal averages, but the actual storms that matter.

What I Did

  • Extracted 10-year weekly precipitation data (2016-2025) revealing a highly right-skewed distribution (Skewness = 2.85)
  • Applied log1p transformation to stabilize variance
  • Identified a clear 52-week annual cycle for seasonal modeling

Models Compared

  • LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory): MAE 0.9675, RMSE 1.7596: best at capturing extreme precipitation peaks
  • BSTS (Bayesian Structural Time Series): MAE 0.72, RMSE 1.45: accurate for weekly trends but too "smooth" for extremes
  • SARIMA: MAE 2.92, RMSE 4.76
  • ARIMA: MAE 3.04, RMSE 4.30

Key Finding

While BSTS is accurate for seasonal trends, it is too conservative for risk management. LSTM is the superior model because it captures the stochastic volatility and precipitation peaks that other models miss. Since predicting storms is more critical for agriculture than tracking averages, LSTM is the right architecture for high-stakes forecasting.

Validation

5-year Rolling Cross-Validation (2021-2025) with Log-Min-Max preprocessing and Adam/MCMC optimization.
